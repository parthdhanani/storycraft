## Slide: The question your teacher never answered

[Narration] If you are in a Class 7 or Class 8 math classroom, you have been told, more than once, that a negative number multiplied by another negative number gives you a positive number.

You have probably also been told that this is just a rule. That mathematicians decided it works this way and we follow along.

This is not true. There is a reason. It is not a hard reason. It is the kind of reason that, once you see it, you cannot un-see — and the next time someone tells you it is "just a rule," you will know they are giving up.

By the end of this short module, you will be able to explain it to someone else. That is the test of whether you've really understood it.

## Slide: Let's start with what we already know

[Narration] We will not jump to negatives. We will walk there.

You already believe — without anyone needing to tell you — that:

- 3 × 4 = 12
- 3 × 0 = 0
- 0 × anything = 0

We will use only these things, plus one more idea that you also already believe even if nobody has named it for you: the **distributive property**. Which is just a fancy name for the thing where you can split a multiplication across an addition.

For example: 3 × (4 + 2) is the same as 3 × 4 + 3 × 2. You can check it: 3 × 6 = 18, and 12 + 6 = 18. Same answer. Always.

That's it. That's all we need.

## Slide: The first step — what does a positive times a negative do?

[Narration] Let's figure out what 3 × (−4) should be.

Here is the trick. We know that 4 + (−4) = 0. That's just what "negative" means: it cancels.

Now multiply both sides by 3:

3 × (4 + (−4)) = 3 × 0 = 0

So 3 × (4 + (−4)) is equal to 0. That's a fact.

But by the distributive property, we can also split it:

3 × (4 + (−4)) = 3 × 4 + 3 × (−4) = 12 + 3 × (−4)

Both sides have to equal 0. So:

12 + 3 × (−4) = 0

Which means 3 × (−4) must be −12. There is no other number that, added to 12, gives 0.

Take a moment with that. We did not declare 3 × (−4) = −12. We proved it, using nothing but multiplication of positives and the distributive property.

## Slide: Now the main event — negative times negative

[Narration] We now know that 3 × (−4) = −12. We are going to use this to figure out what (−3) × (−4) has to be.

Same trick.

Start with: 3 + (−3) = 0. This is just the definition of negative again.

Multiply both sides by (−4):

(−4) × (3 + (−3)) = (−4) × 0 = 0

By distributing the (−4):

(−4) × 3 + (−4) × (−3) = 0

We already know (−4) × 3. It is the same as 3 × (−4), which we just proved is −12.

So:

−12 + (−4) × (−3) = 0

Which means (−4) × (−3) must be **+12**. There is no other number that, added to −12, gives 0.

That is the entire proof. The product of two negatives is positive because, if it weren't, the distributive property would be broken — and the distributive property is something we use, without thinking about it, every single time we do arithmetic.

## Slide: One more way to see it

[Narration] Some students remember the proof. Some students prefer the picture. Here is the picture.

Imagine you owe ₹10 to four people. Your total balance is −₹40. That is **4 × (−10) = −40**. A positive number of debts, each negative, totals negative. Easy.

Now imagine someone *removes* one of those debts from your books. You now owe one fewer debt. Your balance has changed by +₹10. That is **(−1) × (−10) = +10**. *Subtracting* a negative is adding.

Negative multiplication is, in a sense, the act of *removing* groups. Remove three groups of "owing four rupees," and you are richer by ₹12. (−3) × (−4) = +12.

Different language. Same idea. Same answer. Some students click with the proof; some click with the metaphor. Use whichever helps you explain it to the friend you sit next to.

## Slide: Why this matters more than you think

[Narration] Most of school mathematics is taught as a list of rules. The rules work. You can pass exams by memorizing them. Many students do.

The students who go further are the ones who learn that every rule in mathematics has a reason, and that the reason is almost always findable with a small amount of patience. The proof you just walked through uses only ideas you knew before you started this module. Nobody had to teach you anything new. They only had to point at the building blocks you already had and show you how they fit.

If you remember nothing else from this 12 minutes, remember this: when someone tells you a piece of mathematics is "just a rule," they are almost always wrong. There is a reason. Go find it.
