## Slide: Before we start

[Narration] This module is about the things you don't say. The pause. The "sounds good." The Slack message you typed and didn't send.

I am not going to teach you to be a better communicator. You already know what good communication is — you've seen it. What I want to do in the next 18 minutes is show you the four places where new managers most often fail to say the thing that matters, and what it costs the people around them when they don't.

There are two scenarios in this module and a quick reflection at the end. There is no right answer most of the time. There is just what happened next.

## Slide: What "didn't say" actually means

[Narration] Communication failures in the manager–report relationship are rarely about the wrong words. They are almost always about the missing ones.

Three patterns account for most of the damage:

- **Implied praise that never lands.** You meant to acknowledge their work. You didn't. They concluded you didn't notice.
- **Implied concern that never lands.** You meant to flag the slip. You didn't. They concluded everything was fine, until the review.
- **Implied permission that never lands.** You meant to tell them they were allowed to push back. You didn't. They stopped pushing back.

In each case, what you didn't say became the message.

## Slide: A scenario, in real time

[Narration] Priya has been on your team for four months. She is competent, conscientious, and quiet.

Last week she presented a project plan to the wider department. It was good. You said "thanks Priya" at the end of the meeting and moved to the next agenda item.

Today she has sent you a Slack message: "Hey — was the plan okay? Haven't heard anything since Tuesday."

You have a meeting in twelve minutes. You have 90 seconds before you need to leave for it.

## Branch: How do you respond?

[Prompt] You have 90 seconds. What do you do?
[Option: Send a quick "yes all good, will discuss in our 1:1" → priya-quick]
[Option: Take five minutes and write a specific response → priya-specific]
[Option: Wait — you'll respond properly after your next meeting → priya-defer]

## Slide: The quick reply
[Id] priya-quick

[Narration] It's polite. It's responsive. It's also empty calories.

The reply confirms that you saw the plan and that nothing is wrong. It does not confirm that you read it, that you noticed the specific thing Priya did differently from what you'd asked for — the cost breakdown by phase rather than by quarter — or that you thought it was good.

Priya now knows the plan was acceptable. She does not know whether it was good. The next plan she writes will be safer.

In a month, when you do the 1:1 you promised, you will have forgotten the specific praise you meant to give. The moment will have passed. Praise has a half-life.

**What you could have done in 90 seconds:**
"Yes — was actually going to say. The phase-cost breakdown was much clearer than how we usually do it. Will use that as the template going forward. Proper chat in our 1:1."

Same length. Different message.

## Slide: The specific response
[Id] priya-specific

[Narration] You spent five minutes. You missed your meeting by three.

You wrote:
*"Plan was good — sorry I didn't say so on Tuesday, the meeting ran over. The phase-cost breakdown was much clearer than how we usually do it; want to use that as the template. One thought on the timeline for phase 3, will share in our 1:1 Thursday."*

This is the message Priya needed. It does three things:

- Confirms the praise — specifically, not generically
- Names the thing she did differently, so she knows what worked
- Telegraphs a forthcoming concern without ambushing her with it on Slack

The cost was three minutes of your next meeting. Your meeting will survive. Priya's confidence in you as a manager who actually reads her work won't, if you keep deferring.

This is the right answer, but it was not the obvious one. Most new managers default to the quick reply. The reflex to be brief is good. The reflex to be brief at the cost of specificity is the trap.

## Slide: The deferred response
[Id] priya-defer

[Narration] You meant to respond after the meeting. The meeting ran into another meeting. You meant to respond at the end of the day. Your evening filled up.

It is Friday now. Priya sent the message Wednesday. You have not responded.

What Priya has concluded, somewhere between Wednesday afternoon and Friday morning:

- The plan was probably fine, or you'd have said something
- Asking the manager directly is not a productive channel
- The way to find out what your manager thinks is to wait for it to come up sideways

None of those conclusions are catastrophic. Each one is a small permanent reduction in the bandwidth of your relationship with Priya. The next time she has a question, she will spend longer trying to figure it out on her own before asking, because she has learned that asking is a thing that doesn't reliably get answered.

There is no recovery from this branch except the one you don't want to make: messaging her on Friday with "hey, sorry — meant to say earlier..." The longer the gap, the louder the apology has to be.

## Check: What's actually being measured here?

[Q] When Priya asked "was the plan okay?", what was she actually asking you for?
[A] Confirmation that the plan was approved.
[A*] Specific feedback that lets her know what worked and what didn't.
[A] Reassurance that you read it.
[A] An invitation to the next planning meeting.
[Feedback correct] Right. "Was it okay?" from a four-month report is rarely about approval. It's a request for the specific signal — what worked, what didn't — that calibrates their next attempt. Generic reassurance answers a question they weren't asking.
[Feedback incorrect] Reconsider. Approval, reassurance, and follow-up are all reasonable readings of the surface question. But a junior report asking "was it okay?" four months in is almost always seeking calibration, not validation. They want to know specifically what to do again and what to do differently.

## Slide: Scenario two

[Narration] Different report. Different problem.

Arjun is one of the senior people on your team. He's been at the company three years longer than you. You are managing him because of a recent reorg.

For the last two sprints, his work has been slipping. Nothing dramatic — a missed handoff here, an estimate off by 50% there. You haven't said anything yet because (a) you are new in this manager role, (b) he is more senior than you in tenure, and (c) you genuinely don't know if these are slips or just the noise floor.

Your skip-level has now asked you, in a 1:1, whether Arjun is okay.

## Branch: What do you do next?

[Prompt] The skip-level conversation is over. You're back at your desk. What is your next move?
[Option: Talk to Arjun this week → arjun-talk]
[Option: Watch for one more sprint before saying anything → arjun-wait]
[Option: Ask Arjun's previous manager what the baseline looks like → arjun-baseline]

## Slide: You talk to Arjun
[Id] arjun-talk

[Narration] Hard, and the right call almost every time.

The conversation does not have to be a performance conversation. In fact it should not be — you don't have enough data for a performance conversation yet. It should be a calibration conversation:

*"I've noticed a couple of things this sprint that surprised me — the handoff to design last Tuesday, and the estimate on the migration ticket. I don't have enough context to know whether that's a normal variation or whether something's up. Wanted to ask directly rather than form a theory in my head."*

You are not accusing. You are not avoiding. You are stating what you saw and asking what it was.

Two things happen in this conversation roughly 90% of the time. One: there is a thing going on — a personal matter, a frustration with a project, a missing piece of context — and you find out about it. Two: there is nothing going on, and Arjun now knows that you actually notice. The second outcome is, quietly, the more valuable one.

What is true in either case: your skip-level will ask you about Arjun again, and you will have an actual answer instead of a guess.

## Slide: You wait another sprint
[Id] arjun-wait

[Narration] Tempting. Often the wrong call.

Waiting feels conservative. You are gathering data. You are not being hasty. You are not making it weird.

What is actually happening: you are deferring the conversation while continuing to form an opinion. By the end of the next sprint, if the pattern holds, you will have a stronger view of the problem and a thinner relationship with which to discuss it. Arjun, meanwhile, has had two more sprints of his manager noticing things and saying nothing. He may or may not feel it. Either way, you are training him to expect that you will not flag things in real time.

Waiting is the right call only when you genuinely don't trust your own read — when the slip is so small you would not have noticed it if not for the skip-level's question. In that case, "watch one more sprint" is honest. In every other case, it is procrastination wearing a hi-vis vest.

## Slide: You ask the previous manager
[Id] arjun-baseline

[Narration] Defensible, with a sharp catch.

Asking the previous manager for context can be the right move. You will likely learn something — "this is normal Arjun" or "this is unusually quiet for Arjun." Either is useful.

The catch: it is also, technically, talking about someone behind their back. If Arjun finds out (he will, often by accident), he will reasonably wonder why you went to his previous manager before going to him. It will land as a vote of no-confidence even if it wasn't one.

The clean version of this move is to call it openly: *"I want to ask your previous manager what your usual baseline looks like — wanted to mention that to you first so it doesn't come at you sideways."*

That sentence is hard to say. It is also the difference between this move being a smart calibration and a small betrayal.

## Check: What's the failure mode in all three responses?

[Q] Across the three options — talking, waiting, asking the previous manager — what is the common pattern of *failure*?
[A] Acting before you have full information.
[A] Avoiding difficult conversations because of seniority concerns.
[A*] Letting your discomfort decide the timing of the conversation.
[A] Defaulting to whatever the skip-level wants.
[Feedback correct] Exactly. The common failure mode is letting how it feels to you — awkward, premature, presumptuous — drive when and how the conversation happens. Each of the three options is sometimes correct; what makes them wrong is choosing them because they are less uncomfortable than the alternative.
[Feedback incorrect] Closer than you think — but read again. The actual through-line is that the *manager's* discomfort, not the *report's* situation, is what determines the timing. Every option is defensible on the facts and harmful when chosen for the wrong reason.

## Slide: Reflection

[Narration] Two scenarios, three patterns of failure, no clean answers.

If you take one thing from this module: the next time you find yourself drafting a Slack message to a report and deleting it, ask yourself which of the three you were about to skip — implied praise, implied concern, or implied permission. Then send the message. Whichever version of it you can stand to send.

The thing you didn't say will be the message they hear. Make the one you do say worth hearing.
