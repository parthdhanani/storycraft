## Slide: What the law says

[Narration] The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013 — commonly called POSH — defines harassment broadly and places the onus of prevention on the employer.

Sexual harassment includes any unwelcome act or behaviour of a sexual nature, whether direct or by implication. The law covers physical, verbal, non-verbal, and visual conduct. It applies regardless of intent — what matters is impact on the recipient.

Common categories:

- Unwelcome physical contact or advances
- Demand or request for sexual favours
- Sexually coloured remarks
- Showing pornography or sexually explicit content
- Other unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature

## Check: Quick check — recognising harassment

[Q] A colleague repeatedly sends jokes with sexual innuendo to a co-worker over Slack, despite being asked to stop. Is this harassment under POSH?
[A*] Yes — repeated, unwelcome verbal/written conduct of a sexual nature qualifies regardless of the medium.
[A] No — Slack messages aren't covered because the law was written before remote work.
[A] No — jokes are protected speech.
[Feedback correct] Correct. The Act applies to workplace conduct on any medium — including instant messaging — and verbal/written sexually coloured remarks are explicitly covered.
[Feedback incorrect] Not quite. POSH applies wherever work happens, including digital channels. Repeated unwelcome sexual content is harassment.

## Slide: You speak up
[Id] moment-speak
[Narration] Direct intervention can be powerful, but only when safe to do so.

Speaking up in the moment can interrupt the behaviour and signal to the new hire that they are not alone. It also puts the senior colleague on notice. However, intervene only if you can do so without escalating risk. If you cannot, document the incident and report it through the IC.

Either way, also follow up with the new hire privately.

## Slide: You check in privately
[Id] moment-check
[Narration] Quiet support is often what the recipient needs most.

A private check-in respects the new hire's agency — they decide whether and how to act. Listen. Don't pressure them to file a complaint, but do share information about the Internal Committee and the company's reporting channels.

You can also report what you witnessed yourself; the law allows bystander complaints in some cases.

## Slide: You walk away
[Id] moment-walk
[Narration] This is a missed opportunity — and a legal grey area.

Walking away preserves the imbalance that made the moment harmful. While there is no individual legal obligation to intervene in every case, the company's prevention duty includes building a culture where colleagues do step in. Long-term, silence enables repeat behaviour.

If you witnessed potential harassment, the best next step is still to inform the Internal Committee — even anonymously if you prefer.

## Slide: The Internal Committee

[Narration] Every workplace with 10 or more employees must have an Internal Committee, or IC, to receive and inquire into complaints.

The IC has four required members:

- A Presiding Officer — a senior woman employee
- At least two members from among employees, with experience in social work or legal knowledge
- One external member from an NGO or association familiar with women's issues

Half the members must be women. The IC must complete an inquiry within 90 days of receiving a complaint.

## Check: Who can complain?

[Q] An intern at the company has been subjected to repeated suggestive remarks by a vendor's representative during client visits. Can the intern file a complaint under POSH?
[A*] Yes — the Act protects every woman at the workplace, including interns, contract workers, and visitors.
[A] No — interns aren't employees, so they aren't covered.
[A] Only if it happens on company premises.
[Feedback correct] Correct. POSH covers any woman who works in or visits the workplace, in any capacity — permanent, contract, intern, or visitor — and applies wherever work is being performed, including client sites.
[Feedback incorrect] Reconsider. The Act's definition of "aggrieved woman" is intentionally broad. It covers any woman at any workplace, regardless of employment status, and applies during work-related travel and client visits too.
[Points] 1

## Slide: How to report

[Narration] If you experience or witness harassment, you have several reporting channels.

You can file a written complaint with the Internal Committee within three months of the incident — extendable by another three months for good reason. If you are unable to write, the IC must help you put the complaint in writing.

Alternatives:

- Confidential reporting hotline — see your HR portal
- Direct email to the IC Presiding Officer
- Anonymous online form (where provided)

Retaliation against a complainant is itself a violation of the Act and the company's policy.
